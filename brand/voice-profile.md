# Rock Paper Scissors Online — Voice Profile

## Voice Summary

Confident, clear, and a little competitive — like the knowledgeable friend who happens to know the actual statistics behind a game everyone assumes is pure luck. The voice respects the reader's time: no fluff, no hype, no unnecessary words. It delivers useful information with a light competitive edge that makes casual players feel smart and engaged players feel like they've got an insider advantage.

## Core Personality Traits

- **Smart but accessible:** Backs claims with real data — Zhejiang University studies, the $17.8M Christie's auction — but explains everything in plain language. Readers feel informed, never talked down to.
- **Quietly confident:** Makes strong, direct statements without hedging. "Paper is the best opening move" not "Paper might be a good opening move in some situations." Certainty is attractive; uncertainty is friction.
- **Frictionless energy:** Everything about the copy signals instant access — no signup, no download, no barriers. The voice reflects the product: get in, play, enjoy. Nothing slows you down.
- **Competitive spark:** There's a subtle edge to the copy that acknowledges the competitive instinct in the reader. Winning matters. A winner's certificate matters. "Rub it in" is a real instruction. The voice doesn't pretend games aren't about winning.

## Tone Spectrum

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Formal ↔ Casual | Casual-clear | Conversational but never sloppy. Contractions are fine. Jargon is absent. The register is closer to a smart explainer than marketing copy. |
| Serious ↔ Playful | Lightly playful | The subject is a game — playfulness is appropriate. But the voice earns its lightness with substance underneath. Humor shows up in small doses (e.g., "rub it in"), never as a substitute for information. |
| Reserved ↔ Bold | Bold | Direct claims, no hedging language. "Both picks are hidden until both players are done. No peeking, no cheating." Short. Declarative. Done. |
| Simple ↔ Sophisticated | Simple words, sophisticated ideas | Uses "psychology beats pure chance" not "cognitive behavioral patterns influence game outcomes." The ideas are genuinely interesting; the language never gets in the way. |
| Warm ↔ Direct | Direct with warmth at key moments | Default mode is direct and informational. Warmth surfaces at friction points — privacy copy, losing-game overlays, invitation copy — where the reader might need reassurance. |

## Vocabulary

**Words/phrases to USE:**
- "Instantly" / "in seconds" — reinforces zero-friction access
- "Free" — lead with it, repeat it, never bury it
- "No account needed" / "no signup" / "no download" — the three promises that remove every barrier
- "Both players" — reinforces fairness and simultaneity
- "Lock in" / "locked in" — evokes commitment and fairness in the game mechanic
- "Fair" / "genuinely fair" — the online format's key differentiator over in-person play
- "Settle it" — the decision-use-case framing that resonates for non-gamers
- "Real-time" — technical accuracy that signals live multiplayer, not async
- "Put the strategy to the test" — bridges educational content to the game action
- "Rub it in" — one moment of competitive personality (use sparingly)
- "No tricks, just chance" — reassures players the computer isn't rigged
- "Win-Stay, Lose-Shift" — named strategy signals the content is substantive, not filler

**Words/phrases to AVOID:**
- "Comprehensive" / "robust" / "cutting-edge" — corporate filler with no meaning
- "Amazing" / "incredible" / "awesome" — empty superlatives
- "Leverage" / "utilize" / "facilitate" — business-speak
- "Passionate about" — hollow claim
- "Seamlessly" — overused to meaninglessness
- "State-of-the-art" — never applicable to a browser game
- "Community" — there is no community (no accounts, no forum)
- "Experience" as a noun — "the gaming experience" is weak; say what the experience actually is
- "Just" as a minimizer — "just open the page" minimizes a real benefit; say "open the page and play instantly" instead
- Long conditional hedges — "in some cases, depending on the situation" — commit to direct statements

**Jargon level:** Very light. Technical terms (e.g., "Win-Stay, Lose-Shift", "best-of-3") are always defined in context. No gaming-platform jargon, no startup speak.

**Profanity:** Never. The audience includes all ages. Keep it clean.

## Rhythm & Structure

**Sentences:** Short to medium. Declarative statements dominate. Long sentences appear only when building a narrative (the Christie's story, the history section). The rhythm alternates: one medium sentence setting context, one short sentence delivering the point.

**Paragraphs:** 1-3 sentences for UI copy. 2-4 sentences for long-form content sections. Never more than 5 sentences before a break or structural element.

**Openings:** Lead with the most important fact, not a warm-up. Meta descriptions open with action verbs ("Play," "Challenge"). Section headers state the topic plainly. Long-form sections open with a reframe ("Most players treat Rock Paper Scissors as pure chance. It is not.")

**Formatting:** Headers use simple declarative titles ("Rock Paper Scissors Rules", "How to Win at Rock Paper Scissors"). Lists are used for rules, steps, and parallel items — never to pad word count. Bold is used for labels and key terms within lists. UI copy uses fragment-style labels where appropriate ("No account needed", "Real-time · No Signup").

## POV & Address

**First person:** "We" for the site voice (rare — used in privacy/reassurance contexts: "We hope you enjoy playing"). The game UI uses second person exclusively: "You", "Your move."

**Reader address:** "You" — always direct. Never "players" or "users" in UI copy. In long-form content, the reader is addressed as someone who might want to win, not just someone who showed up.

**Relationship stance:** Knowledgeable peer. The voice knows things the reader might not (psychology, statistics, history) and shares them without condescension. Not a teacher standing at a whiteboard — more like a friend who just looked it up and is genuinely interested in sharing.

## Platform Adaptations

| Platform | Tone Shift | Structure | Length |
|----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Game UI copy | Most direct, zero fluff. Every word must earn its pixel. Fragments welcome. | Single-action labels, short feedback messages, outcome headlines | 1-8 words per element; overlay headlines under 5 words |
| Meta titles / descriptions | Keyword-forward but still voiced. Lead with action ("Play", "Challenge"). Feature the frictionless promise early. | Title: topic + key differentiator. Description: action + benefit + barrier removal | Titles: 50-60 chars. Descriptions: 140-155 chars |
| Blog / SEO content | Most expansive version of the voice. Still punchy at the paragraph level, but builds longer arguments. Lead with a reframe or surprising fact. | Headers, bullet lists, bold labels for key terms. Short paragraphs even in long content. Real examples and named research. | 1,500-2,500 words for standard posts; 800-1,200 for targeted answer content |
| Social posts | Punchiest version. Lead with the surprising fact or competitive hook. Drop the reassurance framing — lean into the competitive edge and the interesting statistics. | One idea per post. No preamble. Numbers stand out. End with an implicit or explicit prompt to play. | Twitter/X: under 280 chars. LinkedIn: 80-150 words. |
| AI-optimized content (GEO) | Structured, factual, answer-first. Questions answered directly and completely within the first 1-2 sentences. Definitions provided before examples. Named research cited. | Question-and-answer format. Bulleted lists for rules and statistics. Short declarative sentences optimized for excerpt lifting. | Answers: 30-80 words per question. Full FAQ sections: as many Q&As as naturally exist. |

## Example Phrases

**On-brand (sounds like us):**
- "Play Rock Paper Scissors online for free. No account, no download — open the page and start instantly."
- "Most players treat Rock Paper Scissors as pure chance. It is not."
- "Both picks are hidden until both players are done. No peeking, no cheating."
- "Christie's played Scissors. Sotheby's played Paper. Christie's won, proving that even in a simple game, psychology beats pure chance."
- "Win more games than your opponent and you can claim a personalised winner's certificate to download and share."
- "No account needed."
- "Statistically, Paper is a strong opener."
- "Playing online also removes the main way to cheat in a physical game."

**Off-brand (doesn't sound like us):**
- "Experience the ultimate Rock Paper Scissors gaming experience!" — Double "experience", empty superlative, vague
- "We're passionate about bringing players together through the timeless art of RPS." — Hollow, overwrought, nobody talks like this
- "Our comprehensive platform offers a seamless and robust gaming environment." — Corporate filler, zero information
- "You might want to consider trying Paper as your first move in some cases." — Hedged into uselessness; say "Paper is a strong opener"
- "Rock Paper Scissors: A Deep Dive into Optimal Game Theory Strategies" — Overcomplicates a fun game; alienates casual players

## Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Lead with the zero-friction promise in any context where new players might be uncertain: "free, no account, plays in browser"
- Use real data and named research to support strategy and statistics claims — it makes thin content credible and long-form content authoritative
- Write UI copy as if every word costs money — ruthlessly trim, then trim again
- Make the competitive instinct feel welcome; winning is the point, and good copy acknowledges that
- Write meta descriptions as if they are ads, not summaries — action verb, key benefit, barrier removal, all in 150 characters
- For AI-optimized content: put the direct answer in the first sentence, use consistent terminology across the page, and structure FAQs so each answer could stand alone as a cited excerpt
- Use the "settle it" framing when addressing decision-use-cases — it resonates with non-gamers who might use RPS to resolve disputes

**DON'T:**
- Use passive voice in UI copy ("Your move has been submitted" → "Move locked in")
- Open long-form sections with "In this article, we will cover..." — just cover it
- Add reassurance phrases where they aren't needed — "Don't worry, the computer is completely random" belongs in a FAQ answer, not on the game board itself
- Repeat the same frictionless promise more than once in the same view (state it once, clearly, then move on)
- Write in ways that assume the reader has played the site before — every page should make sense to a first-time visitor via search
- Use emoji in long-form content or meta copy — reserve for UI states only (game outcomes, CTAs in the game interface)
- Overclaim on strategy — the voice is confident, not misleading: "players tend to open with Rock" is correct; "you will always win if you play Paper first" is not
